# Abstract

**Beyond the White Leghorn: A Wet-to-Dry Organoid Framework for 
Functional Characterization of Climate-Resilient East African Kienyeji Poultry**

Joy Manyasi Kabaka | ORCID: 0000-0002-2253-3970  
Independent Researcher, Nairobi, Kenya  
Africa Bioethics Network (Genomics & Biotechnology Working Group)  
jmanyasa@gmail.com

---

Indigenous East African kienyeji chicken breeds represent an irreplaceable 
genetic reservoir, shaped by centuries of smallholder stewardship to withstand 
thermal stress and endemic pathogens that commercial breeds cannot tolerate. 
As climate change deepens production vulnerabilities across rural and peri-urban 
communities, rapid functional characterization of these locally adapted assets 
has become a food security imperative.

Existing avian organoid platforms rely on commercial breed genotypes whose 
constrained genetic architecture cannot recapitulate the mucosal immunology, 
interferon-stimulated gene profiles, or host-restriction allelic diversity of 
indigenous African breeds — leaving veterinary surveillance without 
breed-relevant models to anticipate zoonotic spillover risk or validate 
locally appropriate vaccines.

This repository presents a bioinformatics reanalysis of GSE283090 (Sun et al., 
2025), the most comprehensive public avian intestinal organoid single-cell 
transcriptomic dataset available, derived from broiler and layer chickens and 
aligned against a White Leghorn-derived reference genome. By interrogating the 
ANP32A host-restriction locus and interferon-stimulated gene profiles through 
a variant-aware pipeline, this reanalysis exposes the functional gaps that 
arise when commercial breed data is treated as a universal proxy for all chickens.

Together these components constitute a reproducible, low-resource-adapted 
protocol deployable in decentralized African research settings — directly 
serving the rural and smallholder communities whose livelihoods depend on 
kienyeji. The framework embeds a Digital Sequence Information (DSI) governance 
strategy aligned with the CBD Nagoya Protocol, ensuring genomic assets remain 
legally anchored within native research ecosystems and establishing African 
data sovereignty as a foundational design principle.

**Keywords:** kienyeji chicken organoids, breed-specific transcriptomics, 
ANP32A host-restriction locus, veterinary One Health, African data sovereignty, 
DSI governance
